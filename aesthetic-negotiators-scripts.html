Production Details

Testimonial Capture — Zoom Recording Method

Script 3 requires three testimonial clips. The format is a Zoom call where Jason asks each client a series of questions while the client is on camera from their phone or computer. Jason records his screen during the call so we capture both the video feed and the conversational dynamic. This approach gets real, unpolished reactions— which is what the ad needs— without requiring anyone to travel or set up equipment.

Below is the run-of-show for each guest: the conversational questions Jason should ask, the specific soundbites we need to extract for the ad edit, and technical guidance for the guests to get usable footage from their end.

Format & Technical Setup

Each Zoom call should run 10-15 minutes. Jason leads the conversation— the goal is a natural discussion, not a read. The guest should not see the soundbites below beforehand. If they go off-script and say something better, that's the take we use.

  • Jason's setup: Record the Zoom call with gallery view or speaker view, whichever captures the guest's face cleanly. Screen recording the Zoom window is the backup.
  • Guest guidance to send before the call: Find a quiet room with natural light (window in front of you, not behind). Hold your phone at eye level or prop it on a stack of books— no looking down. Landscape mode if possible. Earbuds or AirPods are fine for audio. Close the door.
  • Audio priority: The guest's voice quality matters more than video quality. Earbuds with a built-in mic will outperform the phone's speaker in most rooms. If the room has echo, suggest they move to a smaller space or a car.
  • What we'll extract: Short 7-10 second clips. One or two sentences per guest. The edit will cut between Jason's studio footage and these Zoom clips, so slight quality differences actually reinforce authenticity.

Jason's Interview Questions

Ask these in order. Let the guest talk— don't cut them off. The soundbite usually emerges naturally from one of these prompts. If it doesn't, guide them toward the language below.

  1. 1Walk me through your practice— how busy are you, and how much time do you actually have to research new equipment?
  2. 2Before we started working together, where were you getting your information about new devices?
  3. 3What's the difference between working with a device rep and working with someone like me?
  4. 4Was there a specific moment where my input changed a decision you were about to make?
  5. 5What do you think would have happened if you'd gone through with that purchase without my input?
  6. 6How do you describe what I do to other practice owners?
  7. 7Would you have figured this out on your own?

Target Soundbites for the Edit

These are what we need to cut into the ad. If the guest delivers something close enough in their own words, that's the version we use. If they struggle, Jason can paraphrase the gist and ask them to repeat it back naturally. The exact wording below is the fallback— not the target.

Testimonial Slot #1 — The Problem

This guest establishes the pain point: information asymmetry. They were relying on reps and had no independent source. We need one sentence that names the problem.

When I started looking for a new device, all my information was coming from sales reps. I needed someone actually on my side.

Testimonial Slot #2 — The Specific Outcome

This is the Robyn story slot— the $150K manufacturer-going-under save. Ideally Robyn herself tells this. We need the dollar figure, the financial trouble detail, and the outcome in two sentences.

Jason saved us from a $150,000 mistake. He told us the manufacturer was in financial trouble— four months later they went under. We would have been left with a device that no one could service.

Alternative (generic version, any guest):

Jason saved us tens of thousands of dollars on the last device we bought. But more than that— he made sure we bought the right one. He looked at the specs, the alternatives, the warranty. We bought a device that actually does what we needed it to do.

Testimonial Slot #3 — The Ongoing Relationship

This guest sells the retainer model— the idea that Jason isn't a one-time transaction. We need a sentence that makes the relationship sound ongoing and strategic.

He's not just there for one purchase. He helps us think through which technology to phase out and which to bring in. That's the kind of partner every practice should have.
Overview

Executive Summary

Aesthetic Negotiators is launching three Meta video ads to generate Free Insider Access signups from a national audience of med spa owners, dermatologists, and plastic surgeons. This document presents the market analysis that drove the creative strategy, and the three resulting scripts. Each script is structured around Meta's algorithmic requirements— particularly first-three-seconds audience qualification— and differentiated from the only direct competitor in the category, Aesthetic Influence. The goal is not to outsell them on statistics. The goal is to make their approach look like what it is: a transaction. Yours is a relationship.

Research & Strategy

Market Analysis

3.1

The Competitive Landscape

There is one direct competitor in the aesthetic device negotiation category running paid Meta acquisition: Aesthetic Influence. Their parent company (aestheticinfluence.co) positions as a medical aesthetics growth agency— marketing, growth strategy, broader practice consulting. Device negotiation is one product among several. Their dedicated offering lives at device.aestheticinfluence.co.

This is significant. Aesthetic Influence is a generalist agency that bolts negotiation onto a broader service mix. Aesthetic Negotiators is dedicated to negotiation as the only service. That structural difference is one of the cleanest differentiators in the category and informs how every ad is positioned.

Their landing page mechanics
  • $300 paid strategy call, fully refundable as service credit
  • "Guaranteed 10x savings or your $300 back" risk reversal
  • Hero stats: 1,000+ doctors helped, 50+ manufacturers, $42K average savings
  • Pain framing: $30-70K overpayment, bad financing, unfulfilled rep promises, no market fit
  • Manufacturer name-drop: InMode, BTL, Cartessa, Candela, Alma, Syneron, Lumenis, Cynosure, Cutera, Sciton, Rohrer, Aerolase
  • Application-style funnel: form → training → strategy call
3.2

Aesthetic Influence Ad Library Analysis

We analyzed four Aesthetic Influence Meta ads running in March 2026. All four follow the same structural pattern:

  • Audience callout in first 2-3 seconds— "Med spa owners," "Doctors, injectors," "If you're buying..."
  • Pain point with specific dollar number— $30-70K overpayment, repeated across multiple ads
  • Authority stack— former rep, both sides of the table, 1,000+ doctors, 500+ devices, every top manufacturer
  • 10x ROI guarantee— appears in 3 of 4 ads
  • Application-style CTA— click → application → training → schedule call (selectivity signaling)
  • Soft sign-off— "Thanks so much, talk to you soon"

Tonal characteristics: stat-heavy, closer-style, hard-CTA, semi-UGC production with talking head and on-screen captions. The first three seconds of every ad satisfy Meta's algorithmic feedback loop. Their hooks work, structurally— they qualify the audience fast and earn the watch-time.

Where they win

Algorithmic discipline, volume claims, risk-reversal aggression, manufacturer name-drop signaling competence.

Where they're vulnerable

All four ads sound identical. Same speaker, same structure, same tonal register. A sophisticated audience reads them as repetitive. Their stat race also commodifies them— the audience can't tell whether to trust the $42K or the $30-70K, and any new entrant making bigger claims becomes more credible by definition.

3.3

Our Read of Aesthetic Negotiators' Existing Funnel

Three signals from prior conversations and the existing positioning informed our strategy:

  • Signal 1 — Organic content has produced zero leads Peters' own assessment: the messaging needs to be simplified, the audience qualification needs to happen faster, and the broad-expertise framing isn't converting cold traffic.
  • Signal 2 — Previous self-managed Meta spend was wasted (~$1,000) No tracking infrastructure, no audience qualification, no offer alignment. The lesson: get the structural foundation right before optimizing creative.
  • Signal 3 — The membership offer is too complex for cold traffic Three tiers, partner products, banked credits toward negotiation success fees. Peters himself acknowledged the structure can't be sold in 30 seconds. The conversion event for these ads is the Free Insider Access signup— low friction, builds list, allows the actual selling to happen on the consult call.
What we took from the strategy call to inform the scripts
  • "Like a lawyer on retainer"— Peters' own positioning frame for the membership
  • The 500-devices-on-the-market complexity problem (raised by Peters' father, a former surgical instruments business owner)
  • The reinvestment story— what $20-50K in savings actually does for a practice over the following year
  • The "what if?" framing— Peters' own articulation of the consultative tone he wants
  • The "conversation above the rep's head" line— Peters' own writing, his strongest original line
  • Peters' explicit anti-stat positioning— he refuses to compete in Aesthetic Influence's car-salesman lane
  • His 13 years inside the industry as a device rep— the credential that makes everything else credible
  • The Robyn / $150K story from his testimonials— a manufacturer-going-under save that proves expertise more powerfully than any savings stat
